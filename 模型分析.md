# Comparative Analysis of the Three Models

## Model 1: HEVA + LCZ

This is a more **academic and research-oriented** model.

Its core logic is:

**Risk = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability**

In other words, it first evaluates the heat hazard itself, then combines it with population exposure and social vulnerability to identify high-risk areas.

The strength of this model is its **strong theoretical foundation**.  
In particular, LCZ (Local Climate Zone) emphasizes differences in urban built form, such as compact high-rise areas, open low-rise areas, dense low-rise neighborhoods, or green spaces. This makes it very useful for explaining **why certain places are hotter than others**. Therefore, it is well suited for:

- urban heat environment research
    
- urban planning base maps
    
- long-term spatial pattern analysis
    
- mechanism-based discussion in academic papers
    

However, its limitations are also clear. HEVA + LCZ relies heavily on remote sensing and LCZ classification, which are relatively static and updated slowly. If land surface temperature (LST) is used as the main heat indicator, it is also constrained by cloud cover and satellite overpass timing, making it difficult to support real-time warning systems.

So this model is good at answering:

> “Which areas are structurally prone to long-term heat risk?”

But it is less effective for answering:

> “Where should a user go right now to reduce heat exposure?”

---

## Model 2: HRI + Shelter

This is a more **practical and intervention-oriented** model.

Its core idea is not only to assess risk, but also to evaluate the gap between risk and available cooling resources:

**Priority = Risk − Shelter**

That means it does not only ask where it is hot or where vulnerable people are concentrated, but also where **cooling resources are insufficient**.

In your GIS framework, this logic has already been translated into an operational structure:

- **HRI = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability**
    
- **OHSI = Green space area / Population**
    
- **IHSI = POI density / Population**
    
- **OHSPI = HRI − OHSI**
    
- **IHSPI = HRI − IHSI**
    

This is already a very clear GIS workflow.

Its major strengths are:

### First, it uses UTCI instead of only LST

LST measures how hot the land surface is, while UTCI measures how hot it feels for humans. UTCI incorporates air temperature, humidity, wind speed, and radiation, so it reflects human thermal stress much better and is more suitable for real-time or near-real-time meteorological input.

### Second, it uses a 500 m grid, which is more suitable for Shanghai

A 500 m × 500 m grid better matches Shanghai’s “15-minute community life circle” and is more closely related to how residents actually move through the city and search for nearby cooling spaces.

### Third, it incorporates the “resource gap”

This is crucial for an app.  
Users do not simply need a heat map. They need a **decision-support map** that tells them:

- where the risk is high
    
- whether cooling resources are available nearby
    
- which places should be prioritized for intervention
    

So HRI + Shelter is better at answering:

> “Which places are both heat-risky and underprovided with cooling resources?”

That already makes it much closer to the goal of your Shanghai app.

## Model 3: Our Final Hybrid GIS Model for the Shanghai Real-Time Heatwave App

This is the model that best fits your project.

It is not a simple copy of an existing framework. Instead, it combines the strengths of the first two models and turns them into a **Shanghai-specific, real-time, action-oriented GIS model**.

The clearest way to describe it is:

> **HRI + Shelter as the main framework, with LCZ-informed adjustments for local refinement.**

This hybrid model can be structured as follows:

### 1. Risk Layer

We continue to use:

**HRI = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability**

Where:

- **Hazard** = real-time or demo-based UTCI
    
- **Exposure** = population density or real-time human flow
    
- **Vulnerability** = proportion of elderly people, children, and other vulnerable groups
    

### 2. Resource Layer

We divide cooling resources into two categories:

- **Outdoor cooling resources**: parks and green spaces
    
- **Indoor cooling resources**: malls, metro stations, cafés, fast-food restaurants, and other accessible air-conditioned spaces
    

This is a major difference from traditional heat-risk mapping.  
We are not only identifying thermal danger; we are analyzing the **mismatch between heat risk and cooling resource provision**.

### 3. Priority Layer

We generate two priority maps:

- **OHSPI = HRI − OHSI**
    
- **IHSPI = HRI − IHSI**
    

This means the final output is not just a generic “hot zone” map, but two actionable forms of urban heat inequality:

- where outdoor cooling resources are lacking
    
- where indoor cooling resources are lacking
    

### 4. LCZ / Built Environment Adjustment Layer

In this model, LCZ is not used as the main analytical framework, but as a **refinement tool**.

For example:

- compact high-rise zones may intensify radiant heat exposure
    
- older dense low-rise neighborhoods may have weaker ventilation
    
- open or waterfront areas may have different wind and shading conditions
    
- the effectiveness and accessibility of indoor shelters may vary across different built environments
    

So in our model, LCZ does not drive the main decision framework. Instead, it helps make the results more realistic by capturing microclimatic and urban-form differences.

# Why We Chose This Model for Shanghai

This part is very important. The explanation should not simply be “because this model is newer” or “because it is more advanced.” The strongest justification is that it fits the **actual goal, city context, and technical pathway** of the project.

## 1. Because our goal is not just to study urban heat, but to support real-time decision-making

HEVA + LCZ is highly valuable for academic analysis and urban planning, because it focuses on the formation and spatial mechanism of heat risk.  
But our app is not designed to tell users what type of urban climate zone they are in. It is designed to tell them:

- how risky their current location is
    
- who is most likely to be affected
    
- whether cooling resources are available nearby
    
- where they should go for relief
    

So in terms of product purpose, **HRI + Shelter is much better aligned than HEVA + LCZ**.

## 2. Because Shanghai is a flat megacity, and the 500 m grid is more practical than LCZ as the main spatial unit

Shanghai does not have the strong topographic variation seen in mountainous cities.  
That means LCZ is still useful, but it is less suitable as the dominant spatial framework. A 500 m grid better matches the “15-minute community life circle” and better reflects how people actually access nearby cooling resources.

So we are not saying LCZ is unimportant. We are saying:

> In Shanghai, LCZ works better as a refinement layer than as the primary spatial unit.

## 3. Because heat adaptation in Shanghai depends heavily on indoor spaces

This is one of the strongest city-specific reasons.

In Shanghai, people do not only rely on parks or tree shade to cool down. In reality, many people go to:

- shopping malls
    
- metro stations
    
- underground public spaces
    
- cafés
    
- other accessible air-conditioned venues
    

That is why separating **indoor and outdoor shelters** is especially important in Shanghai.  
If we only produce a traditional heat-risk map, we only know where it is hot.  
But once we include Shelter, we can identify:

> “Where is it hot, and where are there not enough accessible cooling spaces nearby?”

That is much more useful for a real-time public-facing app.

## 4. Because UTCI is more suitable for human experience and real-time integration

If the goal is a real-time app, human thermal experience matters more than land surface temperature alone.  
UTCI is more directly related to heat stress on the human body, and it can also be linked to gridded weather forecasts for near-real-time updates.

So this model is not only theoretically stronger for your purpose; it is also **more feasible technically**.
